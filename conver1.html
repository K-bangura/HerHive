Gender-based violence is a difficult but necessary topic to discuss. I know it’s something many of us have experienced, witnessed, or know someone affected by. I think it’s important to start by recognizing that GBV can take many forms—physical, emotional, financial, and even cultural. What comes to mind when you think about GBV?

It’s painful to see because they think it’s normal or that it’s their fault.

Yes, and that fear is real. I work with young women in schools, and it breaks my heart to hear them talk about harassment and violence as if it’s something they just have to accept. They say things like, “That’s just how men are,” or “I must’ve done something wrong.” They’re internalizing this idea that it’s their responsibility to avoid violence, which is unfair and damaging.

Women are told not to talk about it or to handle it within their families, which just silences them more. And then there’s the lack of support. If a woman does come forward, she’s often left to navigate it alone, without enough resources to protect her or help her heal.
